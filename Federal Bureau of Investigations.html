History

The Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) was established in 1908 as the Bureau of Investigation (BOI) under the U.S. Department of Justice. It was created by Attorney General Charles Bonaparte during President Theodore Rooseveltâ€™s administration to handle federal crimes. In 1935, it was renamed the FBI under Director J. Edgar Hoover, who expanded its role in national security, counterintelligence, and crime fighting. Under Hoover, the FBI fought organized crime, communist espionage, and civil rights violations, but also faced criticism for overreach. During the Cold War, the FBI focused on Soviet spies and domestic surveillance. After the 9/11 attacks in 2001, it shifted its priority to counterterrorism. Today, the FBI handles cybercrime, public corruption, white-collar crime, and terrorism threats while balancing civil liberties and security concerns.
